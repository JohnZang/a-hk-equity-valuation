# Buffett-History Forensic Framework

Use this framework when the user wants a hard-nosed investment teardown grounded in Buffett-style business logic, commercial history, and financial reality. The goal is to explain what the business really is, why it won, whether it still earns excess returns, and whether management has treated shareholders well.

Do not tolerate empty language. Every important claim should be anchored in filings, disclosed operating data, industry structure, or long-run financial evidence.

## Required Sections

## 1. Business Facts And Essence

Start with facts from filings.

List:

- Core business segments
- Major products or services
- Revenue mix
- Main geographies
- The customer set: consumer, SMB, enterprise, government, channel partner, or other

Then translate the business into plain language:

- Is it a toll booth?
- An addictive habit product?
- A low-ticket repeat-purchase consumable?
- A mission-critical workflow tool?
- A cyclical capacity seller?

State the business essence in the simplest defensible sentence.

## 2. Economic Engine And Moat

Analyze the economic character of the business.

Cover:

- Capital intensity: how much asset base or reinvestment is needed to earn incremental profit
- Order frequency: one-off project, recurring subscription, repeat consumption, or regulated flow
- Scale effects and where they stop
- Gross margin, operating margin, cash conversion, and working-capital behavior when relevant

Then test whether the company has a real economic privilege.

Required checks:

- Five-year ROE trend
- Five-year ROIC trend when meaningful
- Whether returns exceed a reasonable cost of capital over time
- Most likely moat source: brand, cost advantage, switching cost, network effect, regulation, distribution, or IP

Conclude one:

- Clear excess-return business
- Mixed economics with limited moat
- Commodity or quasi-commodity economics

## 3. Industry History: Origin, Why It Won, Where It Goes

Explain the industry's evolution, not just its current snapshot.

Cover:

- Major historical stages from emergence to now
- Important crises, shakeouts, or regulatory resets
- What customer problem the industry originally solved
- How value migrated over time

Then answer the critical question:

- Why did this company become one of the winners?

Distinguish between:

- Structural advantage
- Superior execution
- Timing luck
- Regulatory shelter
- Capital cycle luck

End by judging the current structure and likely 5-10 year direction:

- Duopoly
- Oligopoly
- Fragmented competition
- Declining industry
- Consolidating market
- Platform transition

## 4. Value Chain Map

Map the full chain and the profit split.

### Upstream

Identify the key suppliers or input sources.

- If upstream is corporate and concentrated, note their margins and bargaining power.
- If upstream is fragmented or resource-based, explain the bargaining mechanics instead.
- State whether the company has pricing power, purchasing leverage, or supplier dependence.

### Competitors

Identify the top three real competitors.

Compare over the latest three years when data is available:

- Gross margin
- Net margin
- Asset turnover or another relevant turnover metric
- Market share or strategic position

### Downstream

Identify the customers.

- If enterprise customers dominate, note their economics and concentration if known.
- If consumers dominate, explain what creates stickiness: habit, convenience, trust, price, ecosystem, regulation, or switching friction.

### Profit Capture

State clearly who keeps the fattest part of the economics in the chain and whether the company sits at a choke point.

## 5. Management Strategy Over Ten Years

Judge management over a long enough window to expose character.

Cover:

- How strategy evolved over the last decade
- Which pivots were proactive versus reactive
- Whether the company anticipated change or merely repaired damage after the fact

Then examine three major commitments or strategic promises from the last decade and compare them with later financial outcomes.

Judge whether management is:

- A consistent executor
- A mixed allocator with some overpromising
- Promotional or unreliable

Also extract up to three public statements that best reveal management's operating style or shareholder attitude.

## 6. Capital Allocation And Valuation Reality

Audit where the cash went.

Required checks:

- Cumulative equity and debt fundraising since listing when relevant
- Cumulative dividends
- Cumulative buybacks
- Major M&A and whether it created value
- Dilution history, especially stock compensation and placements

Answer plainly:

- Is this a business that generates distributable cash for owners?
- Or has it mainly absorbed outside capital while promising future payoff?

Then place today's valuation in long-run context.

- Compare against the company's own history when relevant
- Compare against the quality and durability of the business, not just peer multiples

Finally provide reverse-thesis risk.

List three plausible ways the company could become mediocre or permanently impaired within five years.

## Integration With Final View

Tie the forensic findings back to the investment conclusion.

- A business with strong history, resilient value capture, honest management, and shareholder-friendly capital allocation deserves more confidence in valuation assumptions.
- Weak historical economics, promotional management, or poor capital allocation should reduce confidence even when the stock appears optically cheap.
- If the forensic view conflicts with the near-term valuation upside, explain which should dominate the recommendation and why.
