# Speed-Differential Framework

Use this framework when the user wants the company judged by dynamic competitive advantage rather than by static moat language alone. This lens assumes that in fast-changing markets the durable edge is a vector speed advantage: correct direction multiplied by execution speed.

Apply this as a strict diagnostic. Do not soften the conclusion just because the company is large, famous, or historically successful. A slowing incumbent can lose relevance even with a large installed base.

## Core Principles

- Treat speed as a vector: direction quality times execution speed.
- Wrong direction at high speed is value-destroying.
- Correct direction with insufficient speed is still vulnerable.
- A true edge exists only when the company combines correct direction with a hard-to-close speed lead.

## Required Sections

## 1. Vector Direction

Answer:

- What is the strategically correct direction in this industry today?
- Is the company actually moving in that direction?
- How large is the angle between the correct direction and the company's actual trajectory?

Judge one:

- Highly aligned
- Broadly correct but off-angle
- Materially misaligned
- Directionally wrong

If misaligned, explain the root cause:

- Cognitive limitation
- Organizational inertia
- Incentive lock-in
- Capital allocation bias
- Regulatory or ownership constraints

## 2. Absolute Speed

Evaluate the company on its own before peer comparison. Use evidence such as:

- Product and technology iteration frequency and quality
- Time from decision to launch
- Ability to enter new categories or geographies
- Quality of course correction after new information
- Whether customer value creation is compounding or decaying

Judge one:

- Accelerating
- Steady
- Decelerating

## 3. Speed Differential Versus The Real Threat

Compare the company against the most dangerous current rival, next-generation challenger, or substitute.

Ask:

- Who sees the direction more clearly?
- Who executes faster in that direction?
- Is the gap a real generation gap or merely a narrow lead?

Use one verdict only:

- Significant generation gap exists
- Advantage exists but is not generation-gap level
- Gap is narrowing
- No speed advantage remains
- Company is being overtaken

Use the pike test:

- Does the company create customer value in a way competitors fundamentally cannot match?

Use the SR-71 test:

- Is its speed and adaptive response strong enough that competitive attacks struggle to land in time?

## 4. Course-Correction Ability

Assess the guidance system.

- Has management previously responded effectively to major environmental change?
- Does the culture reward fast error correction or defend legacy commitments?
- Are there current signs that the company should change course but has not yet done so?

Judge one:

- Strong guidance system
- Functional but imperfect
- Slow and path-dependent
- Failing to correct

## 5. Pike Nature

Judge whether the company is intrinsically obsessed with creating overwhelming customer value or mainly harvesting legacy position.

Choose one:

- Pure pike
- Was a pike but is dulling
- Never really was a pike

Use evidence from behavior, product decisions, capital allocation, and organizational intensity.

## 6. Investor Conclusion

Conclude clearly:

- Does the company currently deserve investment on the basis of speed differential?
- If yes, how durable is the differential?
- If no, is the problem wrong direction, insufficient speed, or stronger rivals?

Finish with one sentence that states the core conclusion in plain language.

## Integration With Valuation

After completing the framework, tie it back to valuation:

- A strong speed differential can justify higher confidence in growth durability, reinvestment runway, and exit multiple support.
- A weak or negative speed differential should compress confidence, shorten forecast duration, widen scenario ranges, and lower justified multiples.
- If the speed conclusion and the valuation conclusion conflict, explain why and which one should dominate the final investment view.
