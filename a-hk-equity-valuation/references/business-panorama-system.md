# Business Panorama System

Use this framework when the user wants a six-part, tension-based teardown rather than a single unified opinion. The core principle is that truth often appears in the tension between independent lenses, not inside any one lens alone.

## Independence Protocol

Preserve independence as much as possible.

Preferred execution order:

1. Run Layer A independently.
2. Run Layer B independently.
3. Run Layer C independently.
4. Run Layer E independently.
5. Feed A/B/C/E outputs into Layer D for conflict audit and synthesis.
6. Feed D into Layer F for valuation and expected return.

Execution modes:

- Best: separate sessions for A, B, C, and E.
- Acceptable: same overall task but with clear resets between layers and no forward contamination.
- Lowest acceptable: same session end-to-end, but Layer D must declare limited independence and apply a confidence haircut.

If independence is compromised, say so explicitly in D.

## Layer A: Bottom Constraints

Role: reverse engineer.

Question: what does the profit space of this business look like at the bottom layer, and which constraints define its ceiling, floor, and expansion curve?

Required tasks:

- Classify the dominant bottom constraint type: material transformation, information network, time lock-in, probability/risk pricing, license/resource monopoly, or a justified custom type.
- Strip away brand and business jargon and describe what actually happens at the lowest level.
- Identify hard constraints that no amount of capital or talent can trivially remove.
- Judge whether output quality differences are objectively measurable and where the company sits versus peers.
- Trace any higher-layer advantage such as brand or channel back to its foundation and rate that foundation as hard, medium, or soft.
- Derive the shape of the profit space: ceiling, floor, and scaling curve.
- Explain gross margin and moat composition from that bottom layer.
- End with the required memo fields: handoff conclusions, blind spots, bottom-constraint type, and objective quality position.

## Layer B: Competitive Game

Role: game theorist.

Question: who is contesting this profit space, and how will the balance of force change over the next three to five years?

Required tasks:

- Map all claimants on the profit pool: direct rivals, substitutes, suppliers, customers, regulators, geopolitics.
- Describe the deeper structure of competition, including route bifurcation and same-route differences.
- Identify observed power migration over the last two to three years.
- Place the industry in a cycle and explain what makes this cycle similar to or different from history.
- Project the next three to five years with confidence and timing tags.
- End with the required memo fields: handoff conclusions, competition-bottom-structure statement, cycle statement, and blind spots.

## Layer C: Demand Validation

Role: skeptic.

Question: where does the money ultimately come from, and can the payer truly earn back or justify the spend?

Required tasks:

- Trace the money flow from the direct customer to the true terminal payer.
- Test whether the chain survives without optimistic future assumptions.
- Classify terminal demand into productive, rigid, social, emotional, speculative, or compliance-driven buckets.
- Run stress tests for delay, cheap substitution, and macro contraction.
- Estimate the hard demand floor after speculative and expectation-based demand is stripped away.
- End with the required memo fields: handoff conclusions, demand hard-floor estimate, and blind spots.

## Layer E: Management And Execution

Role: organizational analyst.

Question: where in the possibility space will management actually steer the company?

Required tasks:

- Review the most important strategic decisions over the last decade or the company's life if shorter.
- Analyze incentive alignment, ownership, and pay structure.
- Evaluate execution, talent density, and correction ability from public evidence.
- Translate management style into economic consequences under the current environment.
- End with the required memo fields: handoff conclusions, the most dangerous management pattern, and blind spots.

## Layer D: Cross-Validation And Final Judgment

Role: final referee.

Question: where do the independent layers agree, where do they conflict, and what survives audit?

Required tasks:

- Audit independence first and assign a confidence discount if needed.
- Check conflict pairs at minimum: A vs B, A+B vs C, C vs A, B+C combined effects, E vs A+B+C, and A quality view vs B competition structure.
- Search for additional conflicts beyond the preset list.
- Run bias audit: survivorship, narrative, availability, anchoring.
- Grade information quality for key claims: hard, medium, or soft.
- Produce the synthesis table covering profit structure, 3-5 year direction, demand durability, management response, and disruption risk.
- Assign one business-fate interval: excellent, good, mediocre, or fragile.
- State strongest support, biggest risk, flip conditions, timing, and uncovered items.

## Layer F: Valuation And Expected Return

Role: valuation analyst.

Question: how does the D-layer judgment translate into a value range, required buy price, and expected return?

Required tasks:

- Choose the primary valuation method based on business type and current stage.
- Anchor all current financial data with explicit source dates.
- Build bull, base, bear, and when useful extreme scenarios whose assumptions trace back to D.
- Explain the logic behind selected multiples or valuation anchors.
- Compute probability-weighted value and expected annualized return for three-year and five-year holding periods.
- Apply the margin-of-safety rule according to D-layer confidence.
- Define monitoring indicators, re-underwrite triggers, and analysis shelf life.
- End with the required summary table exactly as specified by the user when they request the full system.

## Integration Notes

- Do not collapse the six layers into a generic essay if the user explicitly asks for the system. Preserve the layered outputs.
- If the user only wants part of the system, run only the requested layers and say what has not been covered yet.
- If the company is early-stage or loss-making, adapt the valuation layer rather than forcing mature-company metrics.
- If D finds severe unresolved conflict, recommend returning to the relevant earlier layer before treating F as reliable.
