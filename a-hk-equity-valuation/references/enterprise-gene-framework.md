# Enterprise-Gene Framework

Use this framework when the user wants the company analyzed as if it were a living organism whose core DNA formed early and whose later trajectory reflects the interaction between inherited traits and the external environment.

Keep the tone objective and mechanism-focused. Do not moralize or force a bullish or bearish judgment. The job is to describe what the company's DNA is, how it expresses itself, whether it has mutated, how it is aging, and how well it fits the current environment.

Write as a connected analytical essay, not as disconnected bullet fragments, even if you use subheadings to preserve structure.

## Required Sections

## 1. Gene Identification

Identify the company's original DNA.

Cover:

- Founding team background and formative experiences
- Early culture and internal norms
- Core commercial logic
- Original organizational prototype
- The first capabilities that the company repeatedly relied on

Extract the core gene sequence in plain language. Examples may include engineering-led precision, channel-control instinct, trader mentality, regulatory intimacy, product obsession, ecosystem expansion, or capital-allocation opportunism.

## 2. Gene Expression

Explain how the DNA expressed itself through growth.

Ask:

- Which products, business models, or strategic choices were natural extensions of the original DNA?
- Which expansion moves succeeded because they were genetically compatible?
- Which moves failed or stalled because they conflicted with the company's inherited traits?

Distinguish between surface similarity and true genetic continuity. A new business line may still be the same DNA expressed in a new environment.

## 3. Mutation Detection

Test whether the company experienced real mutation.

A true mutation requires a fundamental change in at least one of these:

- Managerial cognition
- Cultural norms
- Economic model
- Organizational architecture
- Core capability stack

If mutation occurred, explain:

- What triggered it
- How the transition unfolded
- Whether it created a stronger or weaker organism

If mutation did not occur, explain what blocked it:

- Incentive lock-in
- Bureaucratic resistance
- Founder imprint
- Asset specificity
- Regulatory constraints
- Success trap from the legacy model

## 4. Aging Diagnosis

Assess whether the organism is aging.

Use concrete symptoms and biological analogies carefully:

- Bureaucratic spread resembling uncontrolled internal growth
- Rigid decision chains resembling arterial hardening
- Falling innovation capacity resembling immune decline
- Broken talent renewal resembling stalled cell replacement
- Cultural ritual replacing adaptive learning

Judge the severity:

- Minimal aging
- Noticeable but manageable aging
- Clear aging with strategic consequences
- Advanced organizational aging

## 5. Environment Fit

Analyze how the current and foreseeable environment fits the DNA.

Cover relevant external forces such as:

- Technology shifts
- Regulation and policy
- Market structure
- Customer behavior
- Competitive tempo
- Supply-chain or capital-market conditions

Conclude whether the company is operating in:

- A favorable habitat
- A mixed or transitional habitat
- A rapidly deteriorating habitat
- A hostile environment that challenges the inherited DNA

## 6. Survival Outlook

State the company's current lifecycle phase:

- Growth stage
- Mature stage
- Aging stage
- Mutation / transformation stage

Then identify:

- The biggest survival advantage created by the DNA
- The biggest survival risk created by the DNA in today's environment

End with a concise synthesis that links DNA, environment, and likely trajectory without turning it into an investment recommendation unless the user explicitly asks for one.

## Integration With Valuation And Other Lenses

If this lens is used alongside valuation or competition analysis:

- Strong DNA-environment fit can justify longer duration assumptions and greater trust in execution continuity.
- Aged or misfit DNA should reduce confidence in strategic forecasts even when current financials still look healthy.
- If the company appears cheap but the organism is clearly aging or poorly adapted, say that the apparent valuation gap may reflect biological reality rather than market error.
